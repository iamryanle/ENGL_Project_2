Tailspin



By Christine Wilks

Tailspin by Christine Wilks is a nonlinear work of electronic poetry that serves as a memoir of a former pilot’s experience during World War II and in his household. The high frequency background noise, the sound of utensils scraping on plates, and a heart beating in the background is overwhelming to the audience. The audio of the utensils sound very close to you and almost feel claustrophobic. The sudden ringing and siren noises allow for audiences to understand the grandfather’s condition. The audio recordings of explosions, sirens, and the horrors of war provide an immersive experience simulating the grandfather’s own experience in the war.

The affordances of electronic literature enable readers to experience Tailspin in a myriad of ways. Being a form of hover electronic literature, readers have control of the order and pace in which he/she wants to view a section of text. While Tailspin follows an overall linear path, the text in each “chapter” can be read in any order, and the path the narrative unfolds throughout each chapter is controlled by the reader. Because audio clips are played whenever a spiral is hovered, one could experience the entirety of the work without viewing the written text at all. The control that Wilks gives to the reader can be reflective of the grandfather’s own control of his hearing when he refuses to wear a hearing aid; on the contrary, the nature of the text, lacking no order, also reflects the grandfather’s lack of control over his hearing and trauma from the second World War. Overall, Wilk’s use of audio delivers a fully immersive experience that emulates the grandfather’s condition.
